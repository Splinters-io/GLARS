GLARS

Geo-Legal Access Risk Score: An objective framework for quantifying jurisdictional risks in data sovereignty assessments

Overview

The Geo-Legal Access Risk Score (GLARS) framework provides an objective, apolitical methodology for assessing and quantifying jurisdictional risks related to data sovereignty. This helps organizations make informed decisions about where to store, process, and transfer sensitive data.

Note from the Author

Legal jurisdictions will always impose requirements on data. GLARS isn't about avoiding these realities or just generating a numerical score—it's about creating transparency around risks that materially impact both data custodians and owners.

The critical question: What happens when your data is lawfully accessed? Can you architect systems that require explicit access requests, giving you visibility into access attempts? Or must you accept that data stored in certain jurisdictions may be silently accessed by authorized entities without your knowledge?

True data sovereignty isn't about perfect security—it's about informed risk management.

Risks Realised: Real-World Examples

Meta's €1.2 Billion GDPR Fine (2023)

Meta (Facebook) was fined €1.2 billion by the Irish Data Protection Commission for transferring EU user data to the US without adequate protections against potential US government access. This represents a failure to properly assess jurisdictional risk exposure.

Source: Reuters: Meta faces record EU privacy fine over Facebook data transfers

Schrems II Decision Invalidating Privacy Shield (2020)

The Court of Justice of the European Union invalidated the EU-US Privacy Shield framework, disrupting thousands of businesses that relied on it for transatlantic data transfers. Companies that had not quantified their jurisdictional risk exposure faced significant operational challenges.

Source: CJEU Press Release: The Court of Justice invalidates Decision 2016/1250

Microsoft Ireland Case (2018)

Microsoft challenged a US warrant seeking data stored in Ireland, highlighting the jurisdictional conflicts between US law enforcement access and EU data protection requirements. The case demonstrated the consequences of not anticipating legal access risks across jurisdictions.

Source: US Supreme Court: United States v. Microsoft Corp.

Atlassian's AU$13.5 Million GDPR Non-Compliance Cost (2022)

Australian software company Atlassian reported spending AU$13.5 million on GDPR compliance efforts after initially failing to properly assess the jurisdictional risks associated with processing EU customer data. This demonstrates the financial impact of inadequate risk assessment.

Source: IT News: Atlassian has spent $13.5m on GDPR compliance

TikTok Data Residency Challenges (2020-2023)

TikTok has faced regulatory scrutiny and potential bans in multiple countries due to concerns about Chinese government access to user data. The company has been forced to implement complex data residency measures and establish data centers in specific jurisdictions at significant cost.

Source: Reuters: TikTok launches new data security regime called Project Clover

Medtronic Healthcare Data Breach Fine (2019)

Medical device manufacturer Medtronic faced regulatory penalties after a breach exposed patient data stored across multiple jurisdictions. The incident highlighted failures in cross-border data protection and inadequate risk quantification for healthcare data.

Source: HHS: Resolution Agreement with Medtronic
